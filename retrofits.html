How this data was built — methodology

This tool visualises real home-energy audits, but the numbers go through several steps before they reach the charts. Here is the whole journey in plain terms.

1 · Where the data comes from

Every figure starts from Natural Resources Canada's EnerGuide / Energy Rating System (ERS) open dataset — the official record of home-energy audits across Canada, covering audit years 2004–2025. An audit is a trained energy advisor visiting a home and modelling, with NRCan's HOT2000 software, how much energy it uses and where that energy is lost.

2 · Matching "before" and "after"

Each audit record is tagged as an evaluation type. We treat type D as the initial ("before") evaluation and type E as the follow-up ("after") evaluation. A home is identified by a stable address ID, and we keep it only when it has exactly one before and exactly one after audit, with the after audit dated later than the before. That paired record becomes one pre → post row. A home audited only once is dropped — there is nothing to compare it to.

3 · Making sure it's really the same home

To avoid accidentally comparing two different homes (or a home that had a major addition), a pair is kept only if, between the two audits, the floor area changed by no more than 10% and the house type, number of storeys, and number of dwelling units stayed the same. Pairs that fail these checks are removed before anything is counted.

4 · Putting everything in the same units

The source records each fuel in its own unit, so we convert everything to a common one — kilowatt-hours (kWh) — before comparing: natural gas from cubic metres, oil and propane from litres, wood from tonnes, and the overall total from megajoules. Electricity is already in kWh. Greenhouse-gas emissions come straight from the audit (tonnes CO₂e per year) and already include emissions from electricity using each province's grid mix, so fuel-switching to electricity is accounted for fairly.

5 · Fields we calculate ourselves

From the audit values we derive the numbers you see:

EUI (energy use intensity) = total energy ÷ floor area, in kWh/m² — lets a small efficient home and a large one be compared fairly.
Energy saving % = (before − after) ÷ before. Positive means energy use dropped; negative means it rose (common when a home switches to a heat pump — electricity goes up even as emissions fall).
Retrofit depth, based on how much total energy fell: Shallow = up to 10% saved, Medium = 10–50% saved, Deep = 50% or more saved.
Fuel switch = the main heating fuel changed (e.g. gas → electricity).

6 · What counts as an "upgrade"

Each measure is flagged per home using a sensible threshold, so tiny modelling wiggles don't get counted as real work:

Roof / wall / foundation / floor insulation — the insulation value went up by more than 10%.
Air sealing — air leakage dropped by more than 10%.
Windows changed — the recorded window type is different before vs after.
Heating system changed — the heating fuel or equipment type is different.
Heat pump added — no heat pump recorded before, one recorded after.

7 · Two views, two ways of counting

When you pick a single FSA (the first three characters of a postal code), the tool loads the actual individual audit records for that area and calculates everything live in your browser — that is why you also get the sortable table of individual homes.

When you look at a whole province, doing that over hundreds of thousands of records would be slow, so the medians and chart shapes are pre-calculated once with a Python script and shipped as a compact summary, broken down by house type. Both paths use the same bucket widths and definitions, so the two views agree.

8 · How the histograms are built

Charts that show a distribution group values into fixed-width "buckets" and count how many homes fall in each — for example energy-use buckets are 20 kWh/m² wide, heat-loss buckets 5 GJ/yr, and the saving-% chart uses 1% steps. The "improvement" charts only count homes that actually got better on that measure, so homes that stayed flat don't dilute the picture.

9 · Things to keep in mind

These are modelled energy estimates from the audit software, not metered utility bills — actual consumption varies with weather and occupant behaviour. Each bar in the fuel chart is an independent median, so individual fuels won't sum exactly to the total. Outliers (e.g. EUI above 500 kWh/m²) are clipped so the charts stay readable. Pre-retrofit solar is rarely recorded in the source, so "solar added" reflects systems present at the follow-up audit and may slightly overcount homes that already had panels. The source data contains no cost information, so this tool cannot show payback or dollar figures.
